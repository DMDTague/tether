1 — Vision

Music has always been an intimate act. The moment you hand someone one of your earbuds, you're inviting them into something personal. Modern streaming killed the physical cord — but nothing replaced the feeling.

Tether brings that moment back. When you open your profile, you're on stage — friends can see what you're listening to and drop in, synchronizing to your exact playback position. It's not a collaborative playlist. It's presence.

2 — Core Concept

The Metaphor

Sharing a wired earbud. When you "Match Their Vibes," you borrow one of a friend's earbuds — you hear exactly what they hear, at the exact moment they hear it. When they skip a track, you skip. When they pause, you pause — completely, immediately, with no buffer.

Two Modes of Connection

The product supports two emotionally distinct use cases, and the design must honor both:

ModeDescriptionTone
ActiveListening together while on FaceTime or a call — the friend is showing you something, a favorite part of a song, a new album drop.Energetic, shared, communicative
Silent / AmbientQuietly connecting to a friend — maybe an old one, maybe late at night. No chat, no pressure. Just presence.Intimate, low-stakes, almost invisible

The UI should never force the active experience on someone who wants the silent one.

3 — Feature Set

3.1 Profile as a Live Stage

  • Every user has a profile that shows, in real time, what they're currently listening to — track, artist, album art.
  • Actively listening users display a subtle live indicator alongside a presence state (see 3.2).
  • Any friend can visit the profile and see the live playback state.
  • Listening activity is only visible to approved friends (see Privacy Model).

3.2 Presence States

The friend list communicates three distinct listening contexts, inferred automatically or set by the user:

StateMeaning
ListeningDefault active state — playing music, skipping around.
Showing you somethingHost has initiated a session and a listener is already joined. Signals intentional sharing, not passive background listening.
In a sessionCurrently tethered to another user as a listener.

These states give the friend list emotional context without requiring status text or custom messages.

3.3 Match Their Vibes (Core Sync Feature)

  • From a friend's profile or friend list, tap "Match Their Vibes" to sync to the host's playback.
  • The app grabs the host's current track + timestamp and commands the listener's connected streaming service to play from that exact position.
  • Sync corrections run every 3–5 seconds to correct drift (event-driven — see 5.1).
  • Listener's playback is slaved to the host — skips, pauses, and track changes propagate to all listeners.
  • The listener can exit at any time without disrupting the host.
  • The overlay displays a passive session timer: "Listening with Maya · 27m" — visible only inside the active session, never stored as a metric or streak.
New in v0.3 — Audio Crossfade on Join
When a listener taps "Match Their Vibes," their current audio fades down over 2 seconds as the host's track fades up. This mimics the acoustic experience of opening a door into a room where music is already playing — a soft step in, not a hard cut.

3.4 "Listening With" Overlay

  • While in a session, both the host and listeners see a small, unobtrusive overlay in the corner of the now-playing screen.
  • The overlay shows avatars and names of everyone currently in the session plus the passive session timer.
  • On the host's end: a subtle visual + haptic notification when a new listener joins — never interruptive.
  • On the listener's end: a real-time view of who else is alongside them.
  • Next track preview: the overlay shows "Coming up → [track name]" — one song only. The full queue is never exposed.

3.5 The Pulse

  • At any point during a session, either party can trigger a Pulse — a synchronized moment shared across all connected screens.
  • The Pulse is the digital equivalent of making eye contact during a good moment in a song.
  • Intentional and manual only — never automatic.
  • Pulse expression layers (all optional, user-configurable):
    • Visual: a ripple animation across the album art and screen edges
    • Haptic: a synchronized subtle vibration on both devices simultaneously
    • Ambient: a brief screen dim and album-art glow
  • No text, no emoji, no reaction count. The Pulse is never a metric.
Updated in v0.3 — Pulse Charge
To send a Pulse, the user must press and hold the button for 1.5 seconds. The button visually fills as the charge builds. Upon release, the Pulse fires. After sending, the button enters a 5-minute cooldown — the UI gently resists re-pressing. This enforces intentionality and prevents the Pulse from degrading into a spam gesture.

3.6 Transparent Presence

The app's most ambient feature — and potentially its most distinctive.

  • Mutual opt-in only: both users must independently enable Transparent Presence for each other in settings.
  • When both users are opted in and listening simultaneously, the app silently registers mutual presence.
  • The indicator always shows the listener's name: "[Name] is here." — never anonymous.
  • Transparent Presence only activates during active listening — it is not a persistent status and cannot be used to track someone's location or schedule.
  • This feature is the quietest expression of the core thesis: you don't need to announce connection for it to be real.
Updated in v0.3 — Block & Mute Controls
Sever (Block): Blocking a user makes you completely invisible to them on the platform — your live status always appears as offline. They cannot send you knocks, Time Capsules, or presence signals. Blocking is silent; the blocked user receives no notification.

Mute: A softer option. Muting a friend hides their presence signals and notifications without unfriending them. Your live status remains visible to them; you simply stop receiving alerts from their activity.

3.7 Host Pause Behavior

Resolved in v0.3
When the host pauses, all listeners pause immediately. No ambient sound. No hum. No UI softening. Absolute silence. This is intentional — it physicalizes the shared cord. If the host pulls their end, your end goes quiet too. The overlay updates to show "[Name] paused". The silence is the communication.

3.8 Asynchronous Tether & Time Capsules

Presence across time, not just across distance.

  • While listening, a user can mark a timestamp and send it to a friend: "Start when ready → 3:42"
  • The recipient opens it whenever they're ready. Playback begins at exactly the sender's marked position.
  • The send UI should feel like leaving something for someone — not firing a notification. A quiet drop, not a ping.
  • The message shown to the recipient: "Dylan wanted you to hear this part" with the track and timestamp.
  • No reply required. No read receipt. The gesture is complete when it's sent.
New in v0.3 — Environmental Locks
Async Tethers can now carry an environmental condition that must be met before the recipient can open them. Supported lock types:

Time lock: "Unlock after midnight" / "Unlock after 2:00 AM" — uses the recipient's local time.
Weather lock: "Unlock when it's raining" — reads the recipient's city-level weather at the time they attempt to open it.
Date lock: "Unlock on [date]" — for future moments.

Locked Time Capsules appear in the recipient's feed as a sealed anchor — track and sender are shown, but playback is blocked until conditions are met. The sender sets one lock maximum per capsule. Unlock is passive and automatic — the app checks conditions silently when the recipient taps the anchor.

3.9 Friends & Discovery

  • Users build a friends list via username search or shareable invite link.
  • Friend requests require mutual acceptance.
  • The friend list shows presence state (3.2) and who is offline.
  • Tapping a live friend immediately surfaces their profile/stage.

3.10 Memory Anchors (V2)

Sessions become emotionally persistent artifacts rather than log entries.

  • Each completed shared session generates a Memory Anchor — a minimal object stored privately.
  • Format: Jamie + You · Holocene · 12:34 AM · 48 min · 3 pulses
  • The pulse count is the only metric worth preserving — it measures intentional moments, not passive time.
  • Anchors are searchable by friend, track, or mood tag (see 3.11). No feed, no timeline, no sharing by default.

3.11 Session Mood Tags (V2)

  • After a session ends, the app surfaces a quiet, skippable prompt the next time the user opens Tether — not immediately after the session.
  • Prompt: "How was last night?" followed by a single-word selection: Nostalgic · Heavy · Calm · Discovery · Night drive
  • Tags attach to the Memory Anchor and enable filtering — show me all the Calm sessions with Jamie — without creating a social feed or public signal.
  • Entirely optional. Skipping has no consequence.

3.12 Distance Layer (V2, opt-in)

  • Both users in a session must independently opt in for distance to be shown.
  • Displays city-level only — never street or neighborhood: "Philadelphia ↔ Montevideo" or "Same city"
  • Lives in the Memory Anchor after the session, not in the active overlay — it's a reflection on connection, not a real-time surveillance element.
  • No exact coordinates, no distance in miles. The point is the poetry of it, not the precision.

4 — Privacy Model

Music listening is deeply personal. Consent is non-negotiable.

SettingDescription
Open DoorFriends can drop into your session without a prompt. You'll see a notification they joined, but no approval needed.
Knock FirstFriends send a join request; host must accept before sync begins. Default.
Ghost ModeYou are listening privately. No one sees your live status. You can still drop into others' Open Door sessions or send knocks.
  • Users can toggle their mode at any time from the now-playing screen.
  • Specific friends can be blocked (Sever) or muted — see 3.6 for details.

5 — Technical Architecture

5.1 Playback Layer Strategy — Event-Driven

Tether operates as a coordination layer on top of existing streaming services — not a streaming service itself. It does not serve audio.

Supported platforms (V1): Spotify (via Spotify Connect API), Apple Music (via MusicKit).

Updated in v0.3 — Event-Driven Architecture
The v0.2 polling strategy (~500ms Spotify API calls) has been replaced with an event-driven model to avoid rate limit violations:

1. Local clock as source of truth. When the host begins playing a track, Tether records the exact millisecond the track started (track_start_epoch). Between events, the Tether backend calculates the host's current position as now - track_start_epoch — no API call needed.

2. API only on events. Tether only contacts the Spotify or Apple Music API when a meaningful event occurs: skip, pause, resume, or volume change. These are infrequent and well within rate limits.

3. Listener sync. When a listener joins or re-syncs, Tether fires a seek command using the calculated timestamp. Drift correction still runs every 3–5 seconds but uses the local clock, not a fresh API poll.

4. Cross-platform matching. Tracks are still matched via ISRC code. If a track doesn't exist on the listener's platform, the app surfaces a passive following state with track info and no silent failures.

5.2 Frontend

  • React (web) / React Native (mobile)
  • Real-time state via WebSockets — friend presence, playback state, session participants
  • Optimistic UI updates for instantaneous feel

5.3 Backend

  • Python (FastAPI or Django)
  • PostgreSQL — user profiles, friend relationships, session history, privacy settings
  • Redis — real-time presence state, playback snapshots, session membership
  • WebSocket server — live updates pushed to all connected clients
  • Weather API (OpenWeather or similar) — for Time Capsule environmental locks

5.4 Key Technical Risk

Tether is dependent on Spotify and Apple Music API access. Spotify in particular has a history of restricting third-party playback control. This is the primary platform risk. The event-driven architecture (5.1) significantly reduces exposure. Long-term mitigation: build a native playback layer, at the cost of music licensing complexity.

7 — Design Principles

  1. Presence over performance. The app should feel like being in the same room, not like broadcasting.
  2. The silent experience is as important as the active one. No feature should force interaction on someone who just wants to be near someone.
  3. Consent is structural. Privacy controls are not buried in settings — they are a first-class part of the listening UI. Blocking and muting are core features, not afterthoughts.
  4. The Pulse is sacred. It must never become a notification, a reaction carousel, or a metric. It is a single, meaningful gesture — and the charge mechanic enforces that.
  5. Invisible sync. The technical complexity of cross-platform sync should be completely invisible to the user. If it requires a loading screen or an explanation, it has failed.
  6. Quiet by default. The app's baseline behavior is low-weight: minimal animations, muted notifications, ambient defaults. It does not need a Late Night Mode — it should already feel like one.
  7. Depth over width. Every proposed addition should deepen the experience of shared presence, not widen Tether's scope. The question is never "what else could this app do?" — it's "does this make the cord feel more real?"
  8. The cord has weight. When the host pauses, the listener feels it completely. No cushioning. The connection is real enough to hurt a little when it goes quiet.

What Tether will never be

The following features are permanently out of scope — not because they are technically difficult, but because they contradict the product's core thesis:

  • Chat, comments, or any in-session text
  • Reactions, likes, or emoji responses
  • Public feeds or discovery of strangers
  • Follower counts or public profiles
  • Algorithmic recommendations
  • Streaks, achievements, or engagement metrics of any kind
  • Any feature that makes private listening into a performance

8 — Out of Scope (V1)

  • Group sessions with more than ~10 concurrent listeners
  • Voice or chat built into the app — use FaceTime or a call alongside
  • Algorithmic recommendations based on friend listening data
  • Public rooms or discovery of strangers' sessions
  • Native streaming (music licensing complexity)

9 — Open Questions

What happens when the host pauses? Resolved

Hard stop. Music stops immediately on all listener devices. The overlay updates to show "[Name] paused." No ambient fill. The silence is intentional and communicative. When the host resumes, listeners re-sync from the same position.

Cross-platform error state

If the listener uses Spotify and the host plays a track not on Spotify, what's the fallback? Current thinking: show track info and album art (passive following) with a quiet prompt to switch platforms. Never block the session silently.

Session size cap

Is there a cap on concurrent listeners per host? A small cap (3–5) may actually serve the product's intimacy thesis better than allowing unlimited listeners.

Async Tether — send UI

The long-press on the progress bar is the current send gesture. Challenge: making it feel like leaving something, not sending a message. Environmental locks add richness but also add UI surface area. Needs UX testing to keep the flow lightweight.

Transparent Presence — anonymity Resolved

Anonymity removed. Presence always shows the listener's name explicitly. Block (Sever) and Mute controls added as first-class features alongside the mutual opt-in.
